Core Physics

Motional EMF: moving conductors generate voltage

A conductor moving through a magnetic field builds an electric potential across the dimension perpendicular to both the motion and the field. That’s the heartbeat of our “copper bullet” idea.

One-line equation

V = B · v · h

B = magnetic field (≈ 30–50 μT near equator) · v = speed · h = terminal spacing ⟂ to both v and B.

Linear in speed. Double v → double V.

Orientation matters

At the equator, v is eastward and B points roughly north, so the charge separation appears between top and bottom of the artefact. Front-to-back taps read ~zero.

Materials don’t change EMF. Copper vs. quartz vs. superconductor affects resistance, not V.

Drag if you draw power

F = I · L⊥ · B

Close the loop and current flows → magnetic drag removes kinetic/orbital energy. Power out roughly equals mechanical power lost: P ≈ VI ≈ F·v.

Thought Experiment

Beaming Mars warm: the unforgiving energy math

Even with perfect lasers, warming and holding Mars at ~22 °C demands planetary-scale power.

Heat content just for the air

E ≈ m · cp · ΔT

With m ≈ 2.5×10¹⁶ kg (CO₂), cp ≈ 730 J·kg⁻¹·K⁻¹, ΔT = 88 K → ~1.6×10²¹ J (ignores ground/ice losses).

To hold temperature (radiation)

P ≈ 4πR²σ (Tnew⁴ − Told⁴)

With Mars’ radius and 295 K vs 210 K → ~4.6×10¹⁶ W (46,000 TW) continuous forcing.

Conclusion: Motional-EMF harvesters are great demonstrations, but nowhere near the energy scale for planetary laser heating. For terraforming, you’d leverage sunlight via atmospheric engineering or in-situ mirrors near Mars.

Applications

UTC-linked lighthouse beacons for science & timekeeping

Synchronized coastal beacons can act as an optical time-transfer and calibration network for space agencies and citizen science.

Time transfer

Δt = (tarr − temit) − ||rrx−rtx||/c

Residuals give clock offset plus atmospheric delay. Two-way exchanges approach sub-ns under good conditions.

Atmospheric calibration

Δt = τclock + τatm(λ, PWV, P, T) + ε

Dual-wavelength pulses (e.g., 532 & 1550 nm) separate water-vapour and aerosol contributions for better tracking & comms.

Spacecraft uses

  • Imager & star-tracker timing checks
  • Clock drift audits on overpasses
  • Optical link test targets

Precision tiers

  • Community: ms–µs (LED + GPS-PPS)
  • Pro: ns (ns lasers + event timers)
  • Metrology: 10–100 ps (combs, two-way)

Safety & logistics

  • Eye-safe beams (e.g., 1550 nm)
  • NOTAM/NAVTEX integration
  • Calibrated trigger-to-emission delay
Design Guidance

“Ideal” speeds by purpose

Science & calibration

Mach 3–7 (≈1–2.5 km/s): measurable signals with survivable hardware; great for v×B validation and magnetometer cross-checks.

Power demonstration

LEO ~7.7 km/s: long (L ~100 m–km) tethers yield 10s–100s V and kW–MW (large systems), but require reboost (electrodynamic drag).

Beyond

Chasing much higher speed adds extreme heating/ionization in air or mission cost in space; increasing h is usually smarter than pushing v.
